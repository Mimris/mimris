On AKM modelling

Introduction

The purpose of this paper is to highlight and explain the basic concepts of AKM (Active Knowledge Modelling) and how these concepts can be used to model work i.e. the activities we need to design, perform and manage in order to produce desired outcomes.

In most cases models of work describe the activities and tasks needed to achieve the intended outcome. Present models are mostly activity charts and swim-lane diagrams. The problem with these models is that they do not consider the properties of the end product, nor the capabilities and workspaces required for effective realization of the product.

AKM addresses these shortcomings by facilitating for multiple approaches and solution alternatives. AKM starts with modelling of the properties and product structures that shape the outcomes or targets and create business value. From this knowledge base the activities needed to be performed are identified, directed and coordinated. AKM also recognizes that the real world is built of endless chains of dependencies, causes and effects. What is one party’s product is often defined by properties that are other party’s products. Think of a car or a plane composed and constructed from multiple components with similar properties that in themselves are sophisticated products (drive train, seating, suspension and chassis).

AKM consists of a set of concepts modelled by the IRTV meta-model, i.e. the relationships between Information (products, outcomes, properties and parameters), Roles, Tasks and Views. The hard part is to go from these relatively abstract concepts to their practical use in context of an engineering or design endeavor such as designing information systems / computer software. The logic and context richness of practical workspaces are modelled to form the knowledge base required for model-generated solutions and workplaces.

One of the cruxes in AKM modelling is Property objects. Outcomes and products are defined by modelling properties, their parameters and objects. Properties are assigned to roles responsible for property satisfaction, modelling and exploitation. They are represented by parameter values in views. Another crux is capturing roles, their workspaces and collaboration spaces. Hopefully the reader has a better understanding after reading this.

With the scene set, enjoy the journey. We have intentionally used hand drawn figures to illustrate that this is work in progress.

Concepts

Products (outcomes) represents the things that we make, use and manage. Examples are Cars, Airplanes, Oil Fields, Oil Wells, Cities, Projects, Investment plans and Decision memos. The list expands rapidly with new technologies, business models and demands.  
Complex products have interdependent properties. A changed parameter value in one property may have direct impact on other properties. Workspace models are required to balance parameter values in such ways that outcomes, values, rules and constraints are satisfied, and the product meets its objective.

The change-driven work management is captured as task patterns and changes and versioning can be transformed into selection of best-fit-solution alternatives. This will minimize the burdens of change management.

Properties describe the most important aspects of a product and to some extent defines the product. Properties can be products themselves in other contexts. Properties are observed and managed by roles in their workspaces (the digital workspaces are formed by the roles), they are placeholders for property parameters and tolerable min & max values as well as dependencies to other objects and their design and engineering task patterns. Properties can be both positive and negative with respect to the desired outcome.

Task patterns define the work to be performed to develop and balance out acceptable parameter values, so the product satisfies its objectives. Dealing with ill-structured business problems such as what should be the next investment there will be many alternative solutions. Some alternatives will optimize single properties such as profitability and risk profile, while other properties may have parameters outside acceptable values. Balancing the parameters quickly and get acceptance from all roles involved can save time and cost and even lead to a product with better capabilities and confidence.

Views provide a visual context for the task pattern under execution as illustrated in the next figure. What we see is a view that captures the properties involved when designing a well. Drilling constraints, Uncertainties and Trajectories are placed in a Workspace model. They could have been placed in their own views, but that would imply more complex task patterns to balance the parameter values (tearing down silos).

Workspaces are conceptual structures that captures the knowledge from practical work in the form of outcomes, targets, collaboration, tasks and properties, and parameter values.
A workspace model defines the context for work by connecting roles, tasks and outcomes.
The workspace is defined by a set of properties and their parameter values that are captured and made available to the roles in views. This takes us to the last point. Workspaces have emergent properties. As work progresses new situations emerge that must be handled and require new interaction patterns. Think about it like this, a person collapses on the street and you and other by passers need to respond. That is an example of a highly dynamic workspace that emerged into your path.

 

Workspaces is one of the most complex AKM concepts so let’s try to make it more tangible using examples.

Our first example is a game of chess with two roles, black and white a board with 32 pieces and movement rules. Then put yourself 10 moves into a particular game and it’s your turn as white. Your workspace is the state of the board defined by the placement of pieces, movement rules, past moves and outcomes as they materialize there and then. When you decide your next move, you need to take into account the space your move create for your opponent as well as for yourself.

Another example is two engineers tasked to find the next best well to drill. The Petec engineer is concerned about finding the most profitable well trajectory. The drilling engineer wants the trajectory that is simplest to construct. Their shared workspace is the specific well, defined by properties like curvature (dogleg), length, length in reservoir, reservoir entry angles and distance to neighboring wells. Their task is to land a set of property parameter values they both can live with, i.e. a design that maximizes value at an acceptable construction cost / risk.

Our third example comes from drilling where a multiskilled team is tasked to drill a hole with defined properties into a semi-known geological structure. You never know when you run into a pocket of gas or hits a fault at the wrong angle. The team roles workspace captures the state of the hole (formation), the state of the drilling machinery and the state of the drilling process. The team’s decisions are made from interpretation of the state captured in the workspace. If the downhole state is a kick (run into hydrocarbons) it’s critical that the situation is understood, and the right measures performed.

Workplaces support the individual users by providing digital placeholders for their work. The workplace can take the form of a dashboard, webpage or software application that the user uses to do their work. Workplaces are designed and adapted to the user's context, roles and workspaces, and competences and skills, and can be customized to serve individual needs and preferences.  Below is a schematic of a Workplace as a container for views, tasks, products, properties and parameters. Outcomes coordination and communication are normally specific tasks and views fitted to the agreed collaboration and management procedures.


Roles is used to define skills and competencies required for a particular type of work including guidelines for training of individuals as role fillers. Roles is the key to design organizational structures and capabilities. Examples; army infantry squad or a hospital surgical team configured for breast cancer surgery. Each role has primary and secondary responsibilities and skills requirements.
Roles decouples individuals from work and simplifies the design of work. Roles can be filled by automates/robots/agents as well as by humans. In the years to come we will see much more collaboration between humans and computerized role fillers which will drive the development of new types of collaborative workspaces. Roles are defined in the corporate knowledge model and are important during recruitment, staffing and training.

Case study

To make this less abstract, lets develop an example using one of Equinor’s business processes where objective is to come up with a list of wells to drill next year. The process attempts answering the question; what wells should we drill next year? In this case the well is the product and the task is to identify the properties that matter for ranking the wells.

We use a toy example, but it shows the essence. Two roles, D&W and Petec are tasked to create a ranked list of wells based on the following criteria: The most profitable well that have property parameter values that both roles can live with. For one of the property values we have a constraint coming from the corporate management system; dogleg should be as low as possible and not exceed 3 degrees turn pr 30m drilled hole. For the other an NPV value above 5 USD million.

D&W Workspace

D&W aims at a solution where the dogleg is within its max tolerance. Petec on the other hand aims at a design that maximizes the well trajectory length in reservoir (as a measure of value and NPV).

 

The Well parameters diagram view shows the interdependency between dogleg and length in reservoir. The big question is, given the actual well and all existing experience, can D&W accept a higher dogleg than 3 degrees? Petec has by simulation shown that by going to 4 degrees the profitability of the Well is doubled.

To answer this question the effects on other properties must be assessed. The key thing is, by identifying the properties that defines the product in context of the decision to be made and present them in a shared view between the roles involved an acceptable decision can be landed reasonably fast.

Let’s assume that D&W can accept a 3,5-degree dogleg alternative, that would give us 400m in the reservoir. Not as profitable as 4 degrees, but much better than 3 degrees. Access to the background data and underpinning models that supports the different scenarios are important. Connecting background data with the impacts is crucial and made possible using property views of tolerable parameters and their interdependencies.

Task pattern automation and Gamification

AKM originates from manual practical work. It tries to capture the complexity that can be observed in a kitchen when meals are made. In the kitchen we find multiple roles assigned specific tasks working toward a common outcome, the perfect meal.

In the kitchen the main challenge is sequencing of tasks in a constrained environment (number of stoves and short time from order to delivery). In other domains the main challenge might be the analytical process that underpins the decision to be made.

Analytical decision-making processes consists of three steps; data acquisition, data analysis determining the best possible course of action and finally, the execution of preferred course of action. Traditional automation addresses the latter, think of the thermostat controlling the temperature in your living room. The analytical process leads to a desired temperature.

Automation of the analytical process is trickier. One approach is to automate creation of options or models of the reality that is used by human experts to devise a course of action.
One way of implementing such automation is to take advantage of gaming. This mean that we frame the problem as a game with rules, rewards and penalties and then we implement the game using smart learning algorithms. Computer based players have demonstrate superhuman skills in games such as Go, Poker and Chess. One approach used is called reinforcement learning.
Reinforcement learning is a concept where a computer program calling an agent plays against itself and learns what are good moves (course of actions) and what are bad moves.

Equinor has in collaboration with WhiteSpace Energy gamified a small part of the well design workflow at Gullfaks. The gameboard is shown below.

From this work we have learned that AKM methodology can help pushing gamification forward. The relationships between AKM terminology and gaming is:

·       Product = Game Objective

·       Properties = Game components & rules

·       Task pattern = Game moves / decisions

·       View = Game board

The main takeaway is the importance of objectives; what outcome / product do we aim for. Secondly, what are the properties that defines the game (components and rules). Thirdly, what game moves and decisions should be automated, hereunder choosing the best fit algorithms. Lastly, the views or gameboard define how the users will interact with the game, and also what other views are needed to embed the task automation into the user’s digital workplace.

 

From programming to knowledge modelling

Software engineers familiar with object-oriented programming understand properties as object attributes that describe important aspects of the object and that contain data. What is less understood is that properties are placeholders for parameters and parameter interdependency, and that these parameters define object types and classes.

Programming languages create objects by language constructs like class and struct. Reuse has been a central mantra in OO programming, and we are happy when we can create reusable concepts represented by Money in the figure below.

Money is reusable because its instance can be used in other objects.

The OO programming techniques are valuable for application development, but it falls short when it comes to industrial and public object-classes of Products, Organizations, Process and Solution (POPS) knowledge.

The challenge with programmed systems is that they don’t capture the broader context. Where does the “price” come from? What rules or negotiations led to the actual price tag?

 

Traditional software engineering does not address this kind of problems. Most applications are calculators with memory where the knowledge of work and outcomes is stored in the brains of the humans.

AKM addresses this shortcoming and provides a tooling that make it possible to capture detailed workspaces and their broader context. The key to that tooling is properties and their relationship to parameters. Most of these relationships are dynamic task patterns.

 

Meta-modelling and Instance Modelling

In order to capture complex dependencies as task-patterns AKM is able to create dynamic user-defined relationships between objects of products, resources, properties and outcome parameters. These rules and relationships are captured as dynamic task-patterns.

 

The modelling of industrial use-cases has proven that collaboration among humans assigned to roles designing and operating these object-structures depend on access to capabilities to create, collaborate and share both meta-models and operating instance models and views.  Modelling practical workspaces and effective person collaborative workplaces depends on cyclic design modelling of outcomes, workspaces and workplaces.

AKM will provide workplaces with evolving views and capabilities, and repositories for managing the data and knowledge needed for repair, reuse and redesign.

 

Conclusion

AKM is a solution design methodology enabling Enterprise Knowledge Modelling, providing capabilities that are based on outcomes (products, operations, and workspaces) and their properties and parameter values presented in interactive views. These are similar things to what is required when automating analytical work through gamification. It can therefore help designers of solutions for any complex collaboration operations.

AKM captures dependencies between human perception of object-types, properties and parameters, and operations by providing the capabilities for collaborative design modelling of these constructs at both work process development and execution.

The document also highlights some the differences and commonalities between object-oriented programming and knowledge modelling. The knowledge of the human mind is captured and shared and enhanced by practicing in digital workspaces.
The differences are subtle but important.
